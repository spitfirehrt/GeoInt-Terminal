// Analytical Framework

How This Analysis Is Made

The question that started this was simple: why do expert forecasts fail so predictably? Not occasionally — but in patterns. The same categories of error, repeated across different events, different theatres, different analysts. Status quo bias. Linear extrapolation. Groupthink dressed as consensus.

The answer produced a framework. It went through multiple iterations — versions that were overbuilt and gave wrong answers, versions that were underbuilt and gave wrong answers, and eventually a structure that consistently surfaces what established analysis misses. Every analysis on this site follows it.

Assume consensus is not merely slow, but systemically blind to emergent forces — emotionally tethered to established frameworks, and overlooking the ruptures that reshape the world.

// The Five Steps
Step 01
Consensus Mapping
Define exactly what the mainstream view is and where it comes from. Tier the sources — institutional consensus, influential secondary voices, dissenting signals. Identify the explicit and implicit assumptions the consensus depends on. These assumptions are the targets.
Step 02
Deviation Hypothesis & Bias Deconstruction
Formulate the challenge: what if the consensus is not just slightly off, but fundamentally wrong? Systematically examine which biases are driving the mainstream view — status quo bias, linear extrapolation, expert echo chambers, narrative fidelity, omission bias. Find where the analysis is protecting itself from uncomfortable conclusions.
Step 03
Suppressed & Emergent Drivers
Actively search for what the consensus is not pricing in. What actors are absent from the narrative? What feedback loops are being ignored? What weak signals could become central drivers? This is where the analytical work is hardest and most valuable — finding the forces that are real but invisible to the established view.
Step 04
Deviation Forecast with Scenarios
Produce a structured forecast: a baseline deviation prediction, an optimistic scenario, and a fragile/downside scenario. Quantify where possible. Assign probability weightings. The forecast must be specific enough to be wrong — vague predictions are not predictions.
Step 05
Falsifiable Watchpoints
Define specific, observable, time-bound markers. Not "tensions will rise" but "PLA exercise tempo exceeds August 2022 baseline by Q4 2026." Every analysis ends with watchpoints that will confirm or refute the forecast. These are logged publicly on the track record page and never edited after publication.
// Biases the Framework Targets
Status Quo Bias
Assuming current trends continue indefinitely, ignoring non-linear change.
Linear Extrapolation
Projecting past performance without accounting for inflection points.
Expert Echo Chamber
Groupthink reinforced within closed professional networks.
Narrative Fidelity
Prioritising a coherent story over contradictory data points.
Omission Bias
Systematically overlooking actors or dynamics that complicate the narrative.
Predictive Moralism
Mistaking desired outcomes for probable outcomes.
Short-Termism
Focusing on immediate trends rather than long-term structural shifts.
Anchoring Bias
Over-reliance on initial information even when contradicted by new evidence.
// What This Framework Is Not

This is not contrarianism. Inverting consensus mechanically is as unreliable as following it mechanically. The framework seeks deviation where the evidence and the structural logic point to it — not as a default posture.

It is also not a model that produces certainty. Every forecast is probabilistic. The three-scenario structure exists precisely because the future is not determined. The watchpoints exist because being wrong publicly, and understanding why, is more valuable than protecting a track record through vagueness.
