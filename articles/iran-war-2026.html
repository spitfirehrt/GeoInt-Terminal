The Prevailing View

What the World Thinks Happens Next

The mainstream view — drawn from Western think tanks, major wire services, and government-adjacent analysts — coalesces around a surprisingly narrow set of predictions. The war ends in four to five weeks. Israel achieves its core objectives: Iran's nuclear programme is physically disrupted, Khamenei is dead, and the Islamic Republic begins to fracture. The Axis of Resistance — Hezbollah, the Houthis, Iraqi militias — is severely degraded, its central patron gone. Russia and China condemn the strikes but do little else. Gulf Arab states absorb Iranian missile strikes and hold. Oil markets spike, then stabilise within weeks. Iran enters a period of political turbulence that gradually resolves, possibly toward something more democratic. Within twelve to eighteen months, a new regional equilibrium takes shape.

"The consensus picture is operationally plausible but strategically shallow. It answers what happens in the first month and assumes the rest will follow. It will not."

The implicit assumptions underneath this view are worth naming explicitly, because they are the load-bearing walls of the consensus — and several are fragile. They include: that the IRGC fragments and yields to civilian forces; that Iran's nuclear ambition dies with the theocratic regime; that Russia and China have no meaningful retaliatory vector; that the Global South's reaction is rhetorical rather than structural; that post-war Iran will be manageable and broadly cooperative with international reconstruction; and that the United States and Israel have a theory of what comes after the bombs stop falling.

Most of these assumptions do not hold under scrutiny.

Where Analysis Fails

The Analytical Faultlines in the Consensus

Every significant analytical failure in geopolitical forecasting shares a recognisable family of errors. This conflict is no exception. The prevailing view suffers from at least seven compounding distortions.

Misreading Iran's Institutions

The Iraq 2003 Analogy Is Wrong

Analysts keep reaching for Saddam's Iraq as the template for post-war Iran. The comparison fails on every structural dimension. Iran has 85 million people, a coherent national identity that predates the theocracy, a substantial civil society, a sophisticated diaspora with institutional competence, and — critically — a parallel state in the IRGC that operates independently of the supreme leadership. The morning after is not Baghdad 2003. It is something without a clean precedent.

Conflating Regime with Strategy

Nuclear Logic Survives the Ayatollahs

The prevailing view treats Iran's nuclear ambition as a property of the Islamic Republic — remove the regime, remove the ambition. This is precisely backwards. The strategic logic driving nuclear interest is structural: Iran faces a neighbourhood containing nuclear-armed Israel, Pakistan, and a potentially nuclear Saudi Arabia, while having been attacked by a US-Israeli coalition that eliminated its leadership. Any successor government — reformist, monarchist, or military — inherits the same security environment and will reach the same strategic conclusion about deterrence.

Missing the Geoeconomic Earthquake

China's Exposure Is Catastrophic, Not Marginal

Western analysis treats China as a passive bystander that benefits from US distraction. The opposite is true. China has suffered a triple shock to its sanctioned-oil trade network — Venezuela, Iran, and Russia disruptions arriving simultaneously. Its yuan-based energy settlement architecture, the cornerstone of its dollar-displacement strategy, has effectively collapsed. The Belt and Road Initiative's land bridge through Iran is severed. These are decade-long strategic setbacks, not quarterly inconveniences. None of this is priced into consensus analysis.

Underestimating Credibility Dynamics

Russia and China Face a "Credibility Floor" Problem

Moscow and Beijing have now watched, in sequence, the US and its partners remove Assad (December 2024), Maduro (January 2026), and Khamenei (February 2026) — all while Moscow and Beijing issued strong rhetoric and did nothing. At some point, passive opposition becomes existentially untenable for the multipolar project. The risk is not that Russia or China directly intervene in Iran; it is that the accumulated credibility deficit creates dangerous pressure to compensate elsewhere, particularly in the Taiwan Strait and along NATO's eastern edge.

Wishful Transition Thinking

Democracy Is Not the Default Outcome

The narrative that Iranians want freedom — and will achieve it swiftly once the theocracy is gone — conflates genuine public desire for better governance with the operational capacity to deliver it. Street protests do not translate automatically into institutional transitions. The 1979 revolution, itself a mass popular movement, took twelve months to resolve after the Shah fled — and it produced another authoritarian system. The IRGC controls Iran's coercive apparatus. The diaspora opposition has legitimacy but no domestic institutional base. The most probable transition runs through the IRGC, not past it.

The Global South Dismissed

The IAEA Confirmed No Nuclear Weapons Program

The strikes proceeded after the IAEA stated that Iran had no active nuclear weapons programme. Combined with civilian casualties exceeding 1,200 — including 180 children killed in the Minab school strike — and attacks on UNESCO cultural heritage sites, this sequence is being processed across the Global South not as a proportionate counter-proliferation operation but as confirmation that US-led international law is selectively enforced. The reputational and institutional consequences of this perception will outlast this conflict by decades.

Short-Term Energy Framing

The Hormuz Premium Is Structural, Not Transient

Oil price modelling focuses on a four-to-eight week spike followed by stabilisation. This misses the permanent repricing that is underway. Iran controls the northern bank of the Strait of Hormuz regardless of who governs in Tehran. Any post-war transitional government — even a cooperative one — retains geographic leverage over 20 percent of global oil supply. War-risk insurance for Hormuz-transiting tankers is repricing permanently, not temporarily. The structural energy premium will reshape trade architecture for years.

Domestic Politics Underweighted

The US Has No Post-War Plan and Declining Public Support

Initial polling showed only 25 percent of Americans supported the strikes, with 43 percent in opposition. There was no congressional authorisation and no prime-time presidential address to the nation. Trump's stated four-to-five week timeline creates a political clock that runs against operational reality. If the conflict extends, if casualties mount, or if Iran sustains its missile barrage against Gulf infrastructure, the domestic political pull toward withdrawal — from isolationist Republicans and the broader left simultaneously — could produce a Lebanon 1983 dynamic: tactical retreat that leaves allies exposed.

Hidden Systemic Forces

The Drivers Nobody Is Pricing In

Beyond the headline errors in the consensus, there are several systemic dynamics operating beneath the surface of public analysis that will prove more consequential than the air campaign itself.

Geopolitical

The Proliferation Cascade the War Triggers

The combined lesson set of this decade — Gaddafi gave up his nuclear programme and was killed; Saddam had no WMDs and was hanged; the Iranian supreme leader was assassinated after IAEA confirmed no nuclear weapon — is being read carefully in Riyadh, Ankara, Seoul, Warsaw, and Tokyo. Nuclear deterrence is emerging, in the minds of mid-size state leaders globally, as the only reliable guarantee of sovereignty against American military power. The strikes solve the immediate Iran problem while potentially seeding a proliferation cascade that produces a more dangerous nuclear landscape by 2035.

Economic-Infrastructure

Central Asia and the BRI Collapse

The strike on Bandar Abbas port — a critical node for both the International North-South Transport Corridor and China's Belt and Road land-sea bridge — has effectively isolated the six Central Asian states. Their southern trade corridors are collapsed. Pakistan faces simultaneous pressure on its western and eastern flanks. These second-order economic consequences — entirely absent from headline analysis — will ripple through global supply chain architecture for years and place severe strain on already-fragile states in the region.

Military-Technical

Israel's Interceptor Depletion Curve

Iran has been producing approximately 100 ballistic missiles per month since the June 2025 war. Israel's interceptor stocks were already under stress in that conflict. A four-to-five week sustained campaign against an adversary maintaining this production and launch rate creates a real attrition trajectory. If Israel is forced to ration interceptor use — shifting from civilian to military/political priority targets — the domestic casualty and political consequences could fundamentally alter the operational calculus mid-war.

Political-Ideological

The Proxy Network Mutates, It Doesn't Die

The assumption that destroying the patron destroys the network misreads how modern non-state armed organisations function. Al-Qaeda survived the fall of its Afghan base and metastasised. Hezbollah, the Houthis, and Iraqi militias are not subsidiaries — they are franchises with their own ideological ecosystems, revenue streams, and political constituencies. Degraded central funding and coordination does not eliminate them; it decentralises them, making them harder to attribute, harder to deter, and potentially more radically autonomous in their operations.

Financial Architecture

The Accelerated Death of the Petrodollar

Paradoxically, the war that was partly designed to preserve US regional dominance may accelerate the structural shift away from dollar-denominated energy trade. Gulf states being struck by Iranian missiles while hosting US bases creates a public legitimacy problem for their governments. Saudi Arabia and UAE populations did not consent to serve as forward operating platforms. Over a five-to-ten year horizon, Gulf diversification away from exclusive US security dependence — toward India, China, and autonomous capacity — will deepen, not reverse, as a result of this conflict.

Normative-Legal

Sovereign Immunity Is Now Void

The targeted killing of a sitting head of state — Ayatollah Khamenei — by a foreign military operation, combined with Trump's public statement claiming he may have "forced Israel's hand" and acknowledging regime change as an objective, has voided the international legal norm of sovereign immunity in practice. The long-term systemic consequence is the acceleration of nuclear weapons pursuit among every leader of a mid-size state who cannot rely on great-power protection. The norm that deterred such assassinations — mutual vulnerability — has been replaced by a stark power asymmetry that smaller states can only resolve one way.

Deep Assessment

Iran's Aftermath — Three Plausible Futures

Iran is not Iraq, not Libya, not Syria. It is a nation of 85 million with 47 years of theocratic institutional architecture, a massive parallel state in the IRGC, substantial civil society, a sophisticated diaspora, and multiple competing power centres. The post-war trajectory is the most consequential and the least seriously analysed element of this entire conflict. Three futures are plausible. Only one is being discussed publicly.

Most Probable · Assessed at 55%

IRGC Provisional Authority — Managed Liberalisation

IRGC mid-level commanders, surviving the decapitation of top leadership, consolidate control using Iran's constitutional transitional council mechanism. Desperate to preserve institutional survival, they make cosmetic but real concessions: suspending theocratic dress codes, releasing political prisoners, promising elections within two years. A prolonged "transitional" period follows — nominally civilian in presentation but effectively military-supervised in substance.

International recognition is extended conditionally. The nuclear programme is formally suspended under verification. Sanctions are eased in stages. Iran does not become democratic in any meaningful near-term sense, but stabilises into a post-theocratic hybrid resembling Egypt post-Mubarak or Pakistan's guided democracy — nationalist, transactional, and still determined to rebuild strategic depth.

China re-engages economically within three to five years, Russia diplomatically. A more pragmatic Iran emerges — less ideological but equally capable of strategic rivalry, and still harbouring the long-term logic that drove its nuclear ambitions in the first place.

55% · 2026–2033 Horizon
Upside Case · Assessed at 15%

Genuine Democratic Transition

The protest movement — catalysed by Khamenei's death and the paradoxical "liberation" moment that foreign strikes create for internal opposition — achieves critical mass. IRGC mid-level commanders fracture; a significant proportion defects to a civilian coalition. An opposition summit gains international traction, producing a transitional government with genuine UN observation.

Elections within eighteen months return a pluralist parliament. Iran signs a comprehensive nuclear deal, joins regional economic frameworks, and begins normalisation with Israel through back-channels within five years. The Middle East enters a genuine détente phase that no analyst currently considers likely.

This outcome requires: conditional amnesty for IRGC moderates, substantial international reconstruction investment exceeding $50 billion, US restraint on victory-lap rhetoric, and no Israeli attempt to further fracture Iran territorially or ethnically. Each of these conditions is individually uncertain. Together they make this scenario genuinely rare but not impossible.

15% · Requires Multiple Favourable Conditions
Downside Risk · Assessed at 30%

Fragmentation and Civil Conflict

No faction achieves decisive authority. IRGC hardliners refuse to yield coercive control. Mojtaba Khamenei — the late Supreme Leader's son and a leading IRGC contender — attempts a new theocratic consolidation. Kurdish forces in the northwest, backed by US and Israeli air support, establish a de facto autonomous zone. Baloch separatists escalate in the southeast. Arab militias in Khuzestan re-emerge.

Iran enters a Libya-pattern decentralised civil conflict. Pakistan seals its western border, producing a regional refugee emergency. The Strait of Hormuz remains under contested control for two to three years. Global oil markets sustain elevated pricing. China's energy security enters genuine crisis. Russia attempts to exploit the chaos diplomatically but lacks the capacity to decisively influence it.

This is the scenario for which no post-war plan exists. It is the one that carries the greatest risk of metastasising outward — pulling in Turkey, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia, and potentially great powers in ways that no current analysis has mapped. Its probability rises directly with every week that passes without a coherent US post-war strategy being articulated.

Regional & Global Power Dynamics

How the Major Players Are Actually Affected

The following assessments address the long-horizon structural consequences for each major actor — looking past the immediate operational phase to the decade-long landscape this conflict is reshaping.

Israel · 2026–2035

Tactical Victory, Strategic Uncertainty

Israel achieves its immediate objectives: the nuclear programme is disrupted, Khamenei is eliminated, the proxy network is degraded. Netanyahu strengthens domestically, with elections moved forward to mid-2026. But the morning-after problem is acute. A fragmented, humiliated Iran with a fresh martyrdom mythology may prove more radicalising over decades than the Islamic Republic as a deterrable state actor. The precedent of targeting a head of state creates dangerous reciprocal vulnerability norms. Israel's economy — already stretched at approximately $55 billion per year in war spending — faces fiscal stress. Long-term: the Saudi normalisation prize draws closer in geopolitical logic, but Palestinian sentiment headwinds intensified by this war make the domestic politics harder on both sides.

Saudi Arabia · 2026–2035

The Ambiguous Beneficiary

MBS reportedly urged the strikes. Saudi Arabia gains the removal of its primary regional rival and theological competitor. But Iranian missiles struck Riyadh and Eastern Province oil infrastructure. The domestic political cost of being seen as a US-Israel enabler while Iranian ordnance kills civilians across the Gulf is not trivial. Long-term: Saudi Arabia accelerates Vision 2030, deepens its India partnership as an economic counterbalance, and emerges as the dominant Arab power by default. The strategic prize — Israel normalisation — faces intensified Palestinian sentiment obstacles that this war amplifies rather than removes.

China · 2026–2040

Catastrophic Near-Term, Strategic Adaptation

China absorbs a triple shock to its sanctioned-oil network — the simultaneous disruption of Venezuela, Iran, and Russian supply chains collapses its yuan-based energy settlement architecture and severs the BRI land bridge. This deals a decade-long setback to RMB internationalisation and forces reversion to dollar-trade exposure for Middle East energy. China's response will be adaptive: deepening Gulf Arab energy partnerships, increasing Russian pipeline dependency, accelerating domestic energy transition, and deploying rare-earth export restrictions as a countermeasure. The Taiwan Strait pressure scenario increases significantly — not as punishment, but as a strategic necessity to restore deterrence credibility — within eighteen to thirty-six months.

Russia · 2026–2035

The Hollowed-Out Patron

Russia loses its second major Middle East strategic partner in fourteen months (Syria December 2024, Iran February 2026). Its "comprehensive strategic partnership" with Iran — signed just months before the war — proves worthless under fire. The credibility deficit with the Global South deepens. Russia cannot project force to protect partners; its economy is under sustained sanction pressure; its military is consumed by Ukraine. The paradox: higher oil prices temporarily boost Russian revenues, sustaining its Ukraine campaign even as its regional influence collapses. Its strategic response will be asymmetric — cyber operations, amplified disinformation in anti-US narratives globally, and probing NATO's eastern periphery for vulnerabilities created by US focus on Iran.

Pakistan · 2026–2032

The Two-Front Nightmare

Pakistan's western border with Iran becomes a zone of compounding instability — cross-border Baloch militancy, sectarian refugee flows, and the disruption of its Iranian trade corridor. Simultaneously, CPEC loses its Iranian node, creating Chinese pressure on Islamabad to compensate for BRI losses through alternative concessions. Pakistan cannot sustain a coherent two-front military and economic posture. The risk of Pakistan-India-China triangulation crisis — India viewing the chaos as strategic opportunity, China pressing Pakistan asymmetrically — is the most underreported systemic risk of this entire conflict and deserves dedicated analytical attention.

Turkey · 2026–2035

The Indispensable Mediator

Turkey occupies a uniquely advantageous position: NATO membership and Western institutional access on one side, deep ties with Russia, the Gulf, and the Muslim world on the other. Erdogan will play all sides — offering mediation for Iran's transitional government, deepening energy diversification as Iranian supply collapses, and positioning Turkey as the non-Western gateway for post-war reconstruction investment. Turkish strategic autonomy within NATO deepens. In diplomatic terms, Turkey is the clearest non-obvious beneficiary of this conflict, and the one most positioned to shape what post-Islamic Republic Iran looks like.

India · 2026–2035

The Calculated Beneficiary

India's short-term disruption from loss of informal Iranian oil channels is real but manageable. India's long-term position improves materially. Its Israel partnership deepens; its leverage with Gulf states eager to replace Iranian regional influence grows; and Chabahar port — India's strategic gateway to Central Asia — becomes more valuable in a post-Islamic Republic Iran seeking reconstruction partners. India's careful non-condemnation of the strikes (neither endorsement nor opposition) positions it as the swing partner for post-war economic architecture. Long-term, India emerges as the most consequential non-Western actor in the new Middle East configuration.

Global South / BRICS+ · 2026–2040

The Legitimacy Watershed

The IAEA's post-strike confirmation that Iran had no nuclear weapons programme — combined with documented civilian casualties and strikes on cultural heritage sites — will be the defining "before and after" moment in Global South perception of US-led international order. The gap between stated principles and demonstrated behaviour becomes impossible to paper over. Alternative institutional architecture accelerates: BRICS development finance, SCO security frameworks, cross-border payment systems that bypass SWIFT. This is the most durable long-horizon consequence of the war — not the fate of any particular regime, but the permanent fracture of the liberal international order's claim to universality and even-handed application.

Tail Risks

Wild Cards — What Could Shatter the Entire Analysis

The following events fall outside the primary scenario modelling — either because their probability is genuinely low, or because they are widely visible but being systematically ignored. Either category can prove decisive.

⚠ Visible Risk — Being Ignored

Iran Achieves a Crude Nuclear Device Under Chaos

A rump IRGC faction operating in a fragmented, internationally unmonitored state has more freedom, not less, to pursue nuclear capability. The absence of regime accountability in a fragmenting Iran makes proliferation harder to detect and deter than under the Islamic Republic's known, monitored programme.

⚠ Visible Risk — Being Ignored

China Initiates a Taiwan Pressure Campaign

Within eighteen months, Beijing launches a significant Taiwan Strait military pressure campaign to restore deterrence credibility after the US eliminating three of its strategic partners without consequence. Not necessarily an invasion — a sustained blockade drill or forced ADIZ clearance that triggers a direct US-China incident could suffice to achieve the signalling objective.

⚠ Visible Risk — Being Ignored

Israel's Interceptor Stocks Reach Depletion Mid-War

Iran's missile production rate combined with a five-week sustained campaign creates a real attrition curve. A depletion event forces Israel to request emergency US munitions resupply, either extending the conflict or creating a premature ceasefire on terms that leave Iran's proxy infrastructure more intact than currently modelled.

◈ Low Probability — Catastrophic Impact

Hormuz Mining Event Triggers Global Recession

Iran or a rogue IRGC faction deploys autonomous underwater mines in the Strait, creating a sixty-to-ninety day effective closure of 20 percent of global oil supply. Oil reaches $150+. A global recession triggers. Emergency central bank coordination fails along existing US-China financial fault lines. The systemic consequences dwarf any prior energy shock.

◈ Low Probability — Catastrophic Impact

Pakistani Nuclear Doctrine Shifts

Instability on Pakistan's western border, combined with Indian opportunism and Chinese asymmetric pressure, triggers a public Pakistani nuclear doctrine review that formally lowers its first-use threshold. Not detonation — but a declared doctrinal shift that resets South Asian deterrence calculus and triggers cascading responses from India and China simultaneously.

◈ Low Probability — World-Altering

Russia Crosses the Nuclear Signalling Threshold

Facing the collapse of both Syria and Iran as strategic partners and watching US forces eliminate state leaders with impunity, Putin authorises a demonstrative nuclear test — the first since 1990 — or deploys tactical nuclear assets to Belarus as a direct signal. This would end all existing arms control frameworks and reset the entire architecture of global deterrence in ways that no current planning accounts for.

⚠ Visible Risk — Being Ignored

Turkey Captures the Post-War Vacuum

Erdogan positions Turkey as the indispensable mediator for post-war Iran's reconstruction, simultaneously leveraging NATO status and Muslim-majority political credibility. Turkey deepens economic penetration of a post-Islamic Republic Iran, emerging as the dominant non-Arab regional power in a development that neither the US, Gulf states, nor Israel have prepared for or planned against.

◈ Low Probability — Epoch-Altering

A Global Proliferation Cascade

The combined lesson set of this decade — Libya, Iraq, and now Iran — triggers a proliferation cascade in which three to five additional states initiate covert nuclear weapons programmes within five years, each independently calculating that nuclear deterrence is the only reliable sovereignty guarantee against the demonstrated willingness of the world's dominant military power to eliminate non-nuclear state leaders. The NPT framework does not survive this.

"The Iran war's consensus narrative is optimistic about military success and naive about morning-after complexity. The suppressed truth is this: military decapitation of a regime solves the immediate threat while potentially creating a more dangerous, unaccountable, and ultimately nuclear-motivated successor environment — while simultaneously fracturing the global order that gave the war its justification. The aftermath matters more than the strikes. It always does."
