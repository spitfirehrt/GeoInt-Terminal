The Prevailing View

What Energy Markets and the IEA Believe

The mainstream energy market consensus — expressed through IEA forecasts, Goldman Sachs commodity desks, OPEC+ communiqués, and the dominant financial press — converges on a single thesis: the Iran war has produced a significant but temporary oil price shock. Nobody disputes the disruption itself. Iranian anti-ship missile activity, IRGC mining operations in the northern Gulf, and war-risk insurance surcharges have already driven Brent into the $105–120 range during the active campaign phase. Tanker re-routing via the Cape of Good Hope has added 12–18 days to East-bound delivery windows. The consensus is not arguing the disruption didn't happen. The consensus argument is narrower and more specific: that these conditions are conflict-phase phenomena which will normalise within 12–18 months of the fighting ending, and that elevated prices represent a temporary war premium to be unwound rather than a permanent repricing of the underlying supply and risk architecture.

"Markets are excellent at pricing known risks in known time horizons. They are catastrophically poor at pricing structural regime changes that accumulate gradually, announce themselves belatedly, and cannot be hedged through conventional instruments. The consensus is pricing a weather event. The data argues for a climate change."

The implicit assumptions underpinning the reversion thesis are load-bearing and fragile. They include: that post-conflict Hormuz transit risk reverts to pre-war insurance baselines once a transitional Iranian government establishes nominal territorial control; that the disruption-phase rerouting through the Cape and Red Sea alternatives reverses as soon as the shooting stops, restoring normal tanker economics; that Saudi Aramco's East-West Pipeline and Red Sea terminals absorb any residual re-routing without permanent infrastructure investment cost; that OPEC+ cohesion holds and Gulf producers use elevated prices to rebuild fiscal buffers rather than exploit them structurally; that the petrodollar recycling architecture — the mechanism by which Gulf sovereign wealth flows back into US Treasuries — survives the war's geopolitical realignments intact; and that the China-led alternative settlement architecture (the petroyuan system) has been set back rather than ultimately accelerated by the war. Each of these assumptions is disputable. Several are demonstrably wrong already, before the conflict has even ended.

The consensus case for post-conflict reversion rests on a single historical pillar: prior Hormuz disruption events — the Tanker Wars of 1984–1988, the 2019 Abqaiq strikes, the 2021 tanker seizures — all produced price spikes that fully unwound. This analogy is being applied to a categorically different situation. Every prior incident was a perturbation within a stable regional order presided over by an accountable Iranian state with identifiable red lines. The Iran war has not perturbed that order. It has eliminated it. Post-war Hormuz risk is not managed by a deterrable sovereign. It is dispersed across factions with no address. That is not a distinction markets are currently pricing.

Where Analysis Fails

The Analytical Faultlines in the Consensus

The energy market consensus for rapid normalisation rests on six compounding analytical failures — each individually defensible in isolation, but together producing a systematic and consequential miscalibration.

Historical Analogy Misapplication

Prior Hormuz Incidents Are Not Precedent for This

The 1984–1988 Tanker Wars, the 2019 Abqaiq drone strikes, and the 2021 tanker seizures all produced temporary price spikes that reverted quickly. Markets cite these as precedent for rapid normalisation. But all of these events occurred within a functioning regional order — a Hormuz that both sides had strategic incentives to keep open, a US Navy with uncontested presence, and a Gulf political architecture that was stable. The Iran war has destroyed the Islamic Republic, decapitated the IRGC command structure, and left post-war Iran's coercive capacity both degraded and unaccountable. The risk has not decreased. It has been transferred from a deterrable state actor to a fragmented, motivated, unaddressable threat environment. This is not an easier insurance underwriting problem. It is a harder one.

War-Risk Insurance Asymmetry

The Insurance Market Is the Real Price Signal — and It Is Permanent

Benchmark crude prices are headline-visible and heavily traded. War-risk insurance premiums on Hormuz-transiting vessels are structural and opaque. The latter is the more reliable signal of actual market repricing. War-risk premiums on Hormuz tanker routes were already running at 0.2–0.5% of vessel value per voyage before the Iran war — elevated significantly from pre-2019 baselines. These premiums do not revert to baseline simply because a peace agreement is declared. They revert when the underwriting community believes the threat architecture has structurally normalised. A post-war Iran with fragmented IRGC factions, active Hormuz mining capability dispersed to non-state actors, and no central command accountability does not underwrite to pre-war premiums. The insurance market is pricing this. The spot crude price is not.

Petrodollar Recycling Omission

The Dollar Architecture Is More Exposed Than Any Prior Period

The petrodollar system — under which Gulf producers invoice in dollars and recycle surplus revenues into US Treasuries and dollar-denominated assets — is the foundational mechanism of US dollar hegemony and the primary reason the US can sustain chronic current account deficits without a currency crisis. This system rests on Gulf producer trust in US protection and alignment. The Iran war has strained that trust in at least three directions: Gulf states absorbed Iranian missile strikes with limited US interceptor support; Saudi Arabia's OPEC+ coordination with Russia has deepened its strategic ambivalence; and China's active courtship of Gulf producers as alternative security guarantors has intensified. A partial de-dollarisation of Gulf oil settlement — even a shift of 15–20% of Gulf trade to non-dollar instruments — would produce US Treasury market effects that no current IEA or Fed model is adequately incorporating.

Saudi Production Calculus Misread

Riyadh Has No Incentive to Lower Prices After This War

The consensus assumes elevated oil prices are a temporary windfall that Saudi Arabia will eventually cap by ramping production to maintain market share. This logic applies to a Saudi Arabia in a stable relationship with the US consumer market. In 2026, Saudi Arabia has absorbed Iranian missile strikes, faces a contested security environment, is negotiating a 123 Agreement under US pressure, and is managing Vision 2030 fiscal requirements at a breakeven above $80/barrel. MBS has every incentive to maintain an elevated price floor, use OPEC+ discipline to sustain it, and leverage the post-war demand premium to accelerate Vision 2030 diversification — reducing Saudi oil dependency over a decade of elevated revenues. The historical assumption that Saudi Arabia acts as the swing producer moderating prices applies to a different Saudi Arabia.

Infrastructure Vulnerability Underestimated

Alternative Routes Are Constrained, Not Unlimited

The consensus cites Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline (capacity: approximately 5 mb/d), the Red Sea SUMED Pipeline (Egypt, capacity: 2.5 mb/d), and UAE's Abu Dhabi-Fujairah pipeline (capacity: 1.5 mb/d) as evidence that Gulf producers can re-route supply around Hormuz if necessary. Total non-Hormuz export capacity is approximately 9–10 mb/d. Total Gulf oil exports through Hormuz run at 17–19 mb/d. The alternative route capacity covers approximately 50–55% of Hormuz volume — and only at full utilisation, with no redundancy buffer, and with pipelines that have not been tested at sustained maximum capacity under combat-elevated stress conditions. The narrative that "alternatives exist" is technically accurate and strategically misleading.

Demand-Side Adjustment Overestimated

The Energy Transition Does Not Arrive Fast Enough to Buffer This

The consensus incorporates an implicit energy transition assumption: elevated oil prices accelerate the move to renewables and EVs, creating demand destruction that limits the duration of any price premium. This is true over a 10–15 year horizon. It is not true over the 24–36 month window in which the Hormuz premium is most acutely felt. EV penetration in industrial Asia — which drives the marginal barrel demand — is growing but is not at the scale required to substitute for Hormuz-routed supply within any near-term pricing window. Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan combined import over 4 mb/d through Hormuz. Their energy transition timelines do not resolve by 2028. They price accordingly.

Suppressed Structural Drivers

The Forces No One Is Pricing In

Beyond the standard analytical failures, a set of second-order structural drivers is operating below the price surface that will shape the energy architecture for a decade — and that current market pricing does not reflect.

Petroyuan Paradox

China's Sanctioned-Oil Network Collapse Resets Its Currency Strategy

The Iran war has simultaneously destroyed the Iranian node of China's yuan-denominated oil settlement network and demonstrated to Gulf producers that Chinese security guarantees cannot protect their interests under US military pressure. This is a severe near-term setback to RMB internationalisation. But the strategic logic driving China's de-dollarisation effort has intensified, not weakened. Beijing now faces a dual imperative: rebuild its Gulf energy supply relationships AND accelerate alternative settlement infrastructure before the next US strategic action eliminates another node. The petroyuan ambition, set back by two to three years in tactical terms, is being reinvested with greater urgency in structural terms. A China-Saudi oil-for-yuan framework, backed by a credible Chinese security presence in the Gulf, remains the single most consequential threat to petrodollar architecture — and the Iran war's destruction of the status quo has accelerated the conversations in Riyadh that produce it.

IRGC Rump Asymmetric Capability

Non-State Hormuz Mining Is the Permanent Tail Risk Markets Cannot Price

Before the Iran war, Hormuz risk was priced against a single deterrable actor: the Islamic Republic's IRGC Navy, with known capability, known doctrine, and known red lines. The post-war environment has dispersed that capability to factions that are not deterrable, do not respond to diplomatic signalling, and have no territorial or economic assets to hold at risk. A single IRGC remnant faction deploying autonomous underwater mines in the Strait — using technology that Iran has developed and tested since 2019 — can achieve supply disruption that takes months to clear and permanently reprices tanker insurance for years. The market cannot price this risk because it cannot model the probability distribution of a non-state actor with no address. It prices it as negligible. It is not negligible.

LNG Architecture Acceleration

The Shock Is Permanently Repricing LNG Infrastructure Investment

Japanese, South Korean, and European energy importers who watched Hormuz transit risk materialise in 2026 are not waiting for normalisation before adjusting their long-term supply architecture. LNG terminal investment decisions made in 2026–2028 — with 20-year infrastructure lifetimes — will be explicitly built around the assumption that Hormuz-routed supply carries a structural risk premium that pre-war infrastructure planning ignored. US Gulf Coast LNG export capacity, Australian LNG, East African LNG (Mozambique, Tanzania), and Canadian Pacific LNG are the primary beneficiaries. Long-term supply contracts signed in the 2026–2028 window will lock in Hormuz-alternative routing for two decades. This is a structural demand shift that the temporary-shock narrative cannot explain.

OPEC+ Cohesion Stress

The Iran War Has Fundamentally Altered the Saudi-Russia Relationship Within OPEC+

Russia benefited financially from elevated oil prices in the immediate aftermath of the Iran war — higher Brent supported Russian budget revenues while its strategic influence in the Middle East collapsed. But Russian and Saudi interests within OPEC+ are now structurally misaligned in ways that were previously managed. Russia needs elevated prices to sustain its Ukraine campaign; Saudi Arabia needs elevated prices to fund Vision 2030. But Russia resents Saudi Arabia's position as the primary US-aligned Gulf beneficiary of the war it partly financed through Iran relationships. OPEC+ cohesion has held through multiple prior stress tests — but those tests did not include a scenario where the Saudi-Russian relationship was simultaneously economically aligned and strategically antagonistic. The alliance is more fragile than the headline production numbers suggest.

Strategic Reserve Depletion

Western Strategic Petroleum Reserve Capacity Has Been Structurally Drawn Down

The 2022 coordinated SPR release — the largest in US history — drew US strategic petroleum reserve capacity from 600 million barrels to approximately 350 million barrels, where it has only partially recovered. A second major Hormuz disruption occurring against a depleted SPR baseline has materially worse buffering capacity than any prior historical supply shock. The IEA's emergency release mechanism has not been fully restocked. Markets are implicitly assuming the SPR backstop is available at 2019 capacity levels. It is not. This is the most concretely measurable structural change in the supply shock response architecture — and it is not reflected in current forward pricing.

Fiscal Break-Even Ratchet

Gulf Producer Break-Even Prices Have Risen Permanently

Saudi Arabia's fiscal break-even oil price — the Brent price required to balance the national budget — has risen from approximately $70/barrel in 2019 to over $80/barrel in 2026, driven by Vision 2030 spending commitments, NEOM infrastructure, and defence expenditure elevated by the conflict. UAE and Kuwait break-evens have similarly risen. The Gulf producer community that historically moderated prices to maintain market share now needs elevated prices simply to maintain fiscal solvency. The structural floor under the oil price has risen by approximately $15–20/barrel relative to the pre-pandemic baseline — and it will not descend without a fundamental revision of Gulf state spending commitments that no government has the political capacity to make.

Monetary Architecture

The Petrodollar Architecture — Stress Points and Failure Sequence

The petrodollar system is not merely a commodity pricing convention. It is the structural mechanism through which US dollar hegemony is maintained, US deficits are financed, and global financial stability is provisioned. Its failure would be a geopolitical event of the first magnitude — comparable in consequence to the 1971 closure of the gold window, but operating through different and less controllable mechanisms. The Iran war has activated the most serious set of stress points this architecture has faced since the 1973 oil embargo.

Mechanism One — Settlement Diversification

The Saudi-China Yuan Pilot: From Experiment to Architecture

Saudi Aramco and CNOOC have conducted yuan-settled oil transaction pilots since 2022. These were exploratory and politically sensitive — Saudi Arabia did not want to provoke a US response by accelerating them. The Iran war changes this calculation. Having absorbed Iranian missile strikes with inadequate US interceptor support, and having watched the US eliminate its primary regional counterweight, Saudi Arabia now has both the grievance and the incentive to advance the yuan pilot toward institutional framework. A bilateral Saudi-China oil-for-yuan mechanism — initially covering 15–20% of bilateral trade — does not require a formal announcement. It emerges through the accumulation of individual transaction decisions. By the time it is visible to Western markets, it will already be structurally embedded. This is the failure mode that destroys the petrodollar architecture quietly, not dramatically.

Mechanism Two — Treasury Demand Erosion

Gulf Sovereign Wealth Reallocation Hits US Treasury Demand

The petrodollar recycling mechanism functions through Gulf sovereign wealth funds and central banks placing oil revenues into US Treasuries, dollar-denominated bonds, and US equity markets. This flow constitutes a structural component of US Treasury demand. Saudi Arabia's SAMA, UAE's ADIA, and Kuwait's KIA collectively hold over $3 trillion in assets, with heavy dollar weighting. A gradual reallocation of 10–15 percentage points of those holdings toward non-dollar assets — Chinese government bonds, European infrastructure, gold, real assets — would remove a structural demand pillar from the US Treasury market at a moment when US fiscal deficits are at peacetime records. The US Federal Reserve's ability to manage this through QE would face simultaneous inflationary pressure from elevated oil prices — a stagflationary constraint that limits its policy response capacity.

Mechanism Three — BRICS+ Settlement Infrastructure

The Alternative Payment Architecture Is Closer to Operational Than Markets Assume

BRICS+ cross-border payment infrastructure — the mBridge platform, the proposed BRICS settlement unit, and bilateral swap line networks — has advanced from conceptual to partially operational since 2022. The Iran war's lesson that dollar-denominated systems can be weaponised through sanctions has accelerated adoption discussions in every non-Western capital simultaneously. A formal BRICS payment system that can process oil transactions outside SWIFT — initially at low volumes but with the ability to scale — represents the institutional infrastructure for de-dollarisation that prior attempts lacked. It does not need to replace SWIFT to damage the petrodollar. It needs only to provide a credible alternative for the marginal barrel in contested markets. The marginal barrel is where price formation occurs.

Mechanism Four — Price Formation Migration

The Brent Benchmark's Credibility Depends on Dollar Settlement

Brent crude is priced in US dollars on ICE London and NYMEX. This dollar-pricing convention makes dollar settlement the default for global oil trade, regardless of the physical buyer and seller's currencies. A successful competing benchmark — the Shanghai International Energy Exchange (INE) crude futures contract, launched in 2018 and already handling 4–6% of global crude futures volume — denominated in yuan, with yuan settlement and Chinese warehouse delivery, represents a slow migration of price formation authority away from dollar-denominated exchanges. Accelerated INE adoption in the 2026–2028 window — driven by Asian buyers seeking yuan-settled supply in a post-Hormuz-disruption environment — compounds the settlement diversification mechanism. The two processes reinforce each other.

Architecture Defence — What Still Holds

The Dollar's Network Effects Remain the Strongest Structural Advantage

The petrodollar system's most durable advantage is not coercive — it is network. The depth, liquidity, and credibility of dollar-denominated financial markets have no near-term substitute. The yuan is not freely convertible. China's capital account controls prevent the kind of deep, open financial architecture that dollar reserve status requires. The euro has repeatedly failed to convert its trade weight into reserve currency status. Gold provides a store of value but not a functional settlement mechanism. None of the alternatives to the petrodollar system offers the complete package of functions that makes dollar hegemony self-reinforcing. The erosion is real but slow. A 10% de-dollarisation over a decade is not a collapse. It is a structural weakening that compounds with each additional stress event — and that raises the cost of the next dollar defence.

The Failure Sequence

Erosion Does Not Announce Itself — It Reaches a Tipping Point

The petrodollar architecture does not fail through a single dramatic event — a Saudi press conference, a UN resolution, a BRICS summit declaration. It fails through the accumulation of individually explicable decisions: a Saudi transaction in yuan, a UAE sovereign wealth fund rebalancing, a Chinese-Russian oil contract denominated in roubles, an Indian refinery locking in rupee-settled Gulf supply. Each decision is rational at the individual level. Together they shift the marginal barrel away from dollar settlement. When the marginal barrel moves, price formation authority follows. When price formation migrates, dollar reserve demand erodes. The tipping point is invisible until it has passed. The mechanism operating in 2026 is not the dramatic end of dollar hegemony. It is the quiet accumulation of the preconditions for it.

Tail Risks

Wild Cards — What Could Shatter the Entire Analysis

The following events fall outside primary scenario modelling — either genuinely low probability or widely visible but being systematically ignored. Either category can prove decisive for energy architecture over the next decade.

⚠ Visible Risk — Being Ignored

Autonomous Underwater Mine Deployment in the Strait

An IRGC remnant faction or Iran-aligned non-state actor deploys autonomous underwater mines in the Strait of Hormuz — technology Iran has been developing since 2018 and tested in modified forms in 2021. Unlike conventional mines, autonomous systems can be activated remotely, are difficult to detect and clear, and do not require a direct attack decision that would invite a US military response. A 60–90 day effective Hormuz closure produces oil at $150+. US SPR is inadequate to bridge the gap. Global recession probability exceeds 60%. The insurance market closes the Hormuz route entirely for a period, forcing an abrupt structural rerouting of 20% of global oil supply. This is not a low-probability tail risk. It is a visible, capability-confirmed scenario that is being systematically priced as negligible because it is inconvenient to model.

⚠ Visible Risk — Being Ignored

Saudi Arabia Pivots OPEC+ Into a Geopolitical Instrument

MBS uses the post-war environment to transform OPEC+ from a production management mechanism into an explicit geopolitical leverage instrument — explicitly linking production levels to US behaviour on the 123 Agreement, on arms sales, and on the Palestinian question. This is not unprecedented: Saudi Arabia did precisely this in 1973. The difference is that in 2026 Saudi Arabia has a stronger bilateral relationship with China, a deeper BRICS+ connection, and a more credible non-US alternative than it did in 1973. A Saudi production cut explicitly framed as a response to US policy is the most direct petrodollar stress test available, and it is available at MBS's unilateral decision. Markets are not pricing this because they have decided it is not in Saudi Arabia's interest. They may be wrong.

⚠ Visible Risk — Being Ignored

Houthi Reconstitution Targets Red Sea LNG Routes

The Houthi movement — degraded but not eliminated — reconstitutes its drone and missile capability in the 12–18 months following the Iran conflict's end, this time targeting LNG tankers on the Red Sea alternative route that Gulf producers are activating to bypass Hormuz. A successful LNG tanker attack on the Red Sea route simultaneously eliminates the primary alternative to Hormuz transit and creates a scenario where there is no safe Gulf export corridor. The insurance market's response — suspending coverage on both Hormuz and Red Sea LNG routes simultaneously — would be an energy supply shock for which no mitigation architecture exists. This is not hypothetical; it is the logical strategic response of a movement motivated by exactly this geopolitical moment.

◈ Low Probability — Catastrophic Impact

Dollar Confidence Crisis Triggered by Gulf Reserve Reallocation

A coordinated Gulf sovereign wealth fund reallocation away from US Treasuries — even 10–15% of combined GCC sovereign wealth — occurring simultaneously with elevated US fiscal deficits and Federal Reserve rate constraints from oil-driven inflation, produces a US Treasury market confidence event. Yield spike, dollar depreciation, and emergency Fed intervention create a stagflationary loop that 1970s policy tools — the last time this configuration occurred — were inadequate to manage. The probability of this being coordinated rather than emergent is low. The probability of it emerging from individually rational Gulf reallocation decisions, without coordination, is meaningfully higher than markets are pricing.

◈ Low Probability — Epoch-Altering

China Achieves Dominant Gulf Security Presence Within 5 Years

Beijing, drawing the lesson that its Iranian partner was eliminated due to its own inability to project protective force, establishes a formal naval presence in the Gulf of Oman or secures a basing arrangement in Oman or the UAE within five years. A Chinese naval facility within Hormuz range fundamentally alters the strategic geometry of the entire analysis: it makes the Hormuz premium a negotiating lever between great powers rather than a market risk to be priced, it changes Saudi Arabia's security calculus more profoundly than any bilateral agreement with Washington, and it makes the petroyuan transition from a commercial preference to a strategic necessity for Gulf states seeking the credible guarantee that the US-Hormuz relationship no longer provides. This is not near-term. But its structural logic is more compelling in 2026 than at any prior moment.

◈ Low Probability — Catastrophic Impact

Abqaiq / Ras Tanura Infrastructure Destruction

A successful strike on Saudi Aramco's Abqaiq oil processing facility — which handles approximately 7% of global oil supply — or on the Ras Tanura export terminal would remove 4–6 mb/d from global markets for a repair period of 6–18 months depending on damage extent. The 2019 drone strike on Abqaiq was only partial; a committed attack with post-Iran war capability dispersal could achieve substantially greater damage. Brent at $180–220/barrel for a sustained period would trigger a global recession of 2008 magnitude, with the additional complication of simultaneous financial market stress from petrodollar system disruption. The Saudi infrastructure is both the most consequential single target in the global energy system and the most directly threatened by the post-war dispersal of Iranian-aligned strike capability.

◈ Low Probability — World-Altering

Petrodollar System Collapse Triggers Dollar Reserve Crisis

A formal Saudi announcement of dollar settlement abandonment — however unlikely as an overt policy action — or the functional equivalent achieved through accumulated de facto settlement diversification crossing 30% of Gulf oil trade, would trigger a dollar reserve currency crisis that no prior financial system stress test has modelled. The US dollar's reserve currency status rests on three foundations: military dominance, deep capital markets, and petrodollar recycling. The Iran war has stress-tested the first, the fiscal trajectory has weakened the second, and the scenario described here would remove the third. The consequence is not merely economic — it is the end of US unilateral geopolitical capacity, which requires dollar hegemony for its financing. This scenario does not emerge from a single decision. It emerges from the quiet accumulation of rational individual choices over a decade. That accumulation has begun.

⚠ Visible Risk — Being Ignored

Energy Transition Capital Disrupted by Elevated Price Signals

Sustained oil above $100/barrel for 24+ months produces a capital allocation paradox: elevated fossil fuel returns draw investment back to oil and gas development, while simultaneously making renewables economics more attractive for consumers. The net effect is a bifurcated capital market — massive renewable deployment in consumer markets combined with resurgent fossil fuel exploration investment — that produces an oil supply overhang in the early 2030s and a renewables overcapacity simultaneously. This structural whipsaw has occurred before (2005–2015). Its consequence — oil crash in 2030 producing Gulf fiscal crises and OPEC+ collapse — is the scenario that breaks the elevated price floor and produces the very normalisation the consensus is prematurely pricing today, but three to five years later and from a much higher starting point.

Three Paths Through the Hormuz Premium Decade

Baseline Forecast · Assessed at 55%

Structural Repricing — The Premium Embeds and Compounds

War-risk insurance premiums on Hormuz tanker routes settle at 0.35–0.55% of vessel value as their new structural baseline. Brent crude finds a new floor of $88–105/barrel with an explicitly identified $12–18 Hormuz risk premium embedded in forward pricing. Saudi Arabia maintains OPEC+ discipline, comfortable with the elevated floor that their fiscal break-even now requires. Long-term LNG contracts are signed at volume by Japanese, Korean, and Taiwanese buyers, locking in non-Hormuz supply for 20 years and accelerating the structural diversification away from Gulf dependency.

The petrodollar architecture does not collapse but it visibly erodes. Saudi-China yuan settlement reaches 8–12% of bilateral trade by 2029. Gulf sovereign wealth funds quietly rebalance 8–10 percentage points of dollar asset allocation into alternative instruments. BRICS+ payment infrastructure achieves operational capability for the marginal barrel in non-Western markets. US Treasury demand from Gulf recycling declines structurally by 2030, raising long-term yields by 40–60 basis points above the baseline that prior petrodollar flows supported. The consensus normalisation narrative is not dramatically falsified in a single event. It simply fails to materialise — the premium is still there in 2028, still there in 2030, eventually recognised as the new normal it became in 2026.

Contained Scenario · Assessed at 25%

Managed Transition — The Consensus Is Roughly Right, But Later

A post-war Iranian transitional government achieves sufficient central authority to bring IRGC remnant factions under nominal control within 18 months. A US-led mine-clearing operation certifies Hormuz clean. Insurance premiums decline toward the 0.15–0.25% range — elevated from pre-war but not permanently repriced at structural crisis levels. Saudi Arabia signs a gold standard 123 Agreement, cementing its security relationship with Washington and reducing the yuan settlement incentive. The petroyuan pilot stalls as Chinese financial architecture credibility limits are exposed.

Brent finds a new floor at $82–92/barrel — elevated from pre-war but lower than the assessed baseline. This is not a full consensus vindication: prices do not return to $75–80. They settle at a level that reflects the genuine structural changes (higher Gulf break-evens, reduced SPR capacity, residual insurance premium) while avoiding the full permanent repricing. The energy transition continues its trajectory. LNG investment increases but not at supercycle pace. The petrodollar architecture bends without breaking. This outcome requires Iranian political stabilisation, credible US security commitments to the Gulf, and Chinese strategic miscalculation in its petroyuan acceleration — a combination of three favourable conditions, each individually uncertain.

25% · Requires Three Favourable Conditions
Escalatory Scenario · Assessed at 20%

Second Disruption — The Architecture Breaks

A second Hormuz disruption event — autonomous mine deployment, Houthi reconstitution attack on the Red Sea alternative route, or a successful strike on Abqaiq — occurs within 18 months of the Iran conflict's end. Brent reaches $130–160/barrel. The IEA emergency SPR release proves inadequate to bridge the gap at 60% of prior capacity. Global recession probability exceeds 65%. The Federal Reserve faces simultaneous inflationary pressure from oil prices and deflationary pressure from financial market stress — its policy toolkit is paralysed.

In this environment, the petrodollar architecture faces its first genuine crisis since 1973. Gulf sovereign wealth fund reallocation accelerates from gradual to urgent. The yuan settlement pilot is fast-tracked to operational scale by bilateral Saudi-Chinese necessity rather than strategic preference. BRICS+ payment infrastructure is activated beyond its tested capacity. The result is not an immediate dollar collapse but a structural inflection point that permanently reduces the dollar's share of global reserve holdings by 8–15 percentage points over the subsequent decade. This scenario's probability is assessed at 20% — elevated, not negligible, and significantly higher than the approximately 3–5% implied by current market pricing of a second major disruption.
