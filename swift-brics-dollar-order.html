The Prevailing View

What Western Economists and Central Banks Believe

The dominant analytical consensus on dollar hegemony expressed through US Federal Reserve research, IMF working papers, BIS quarterly reviews, and the major financial press rests on a thesis that has survived every geopolitical challenge for eighty years and therefore carries the full weight of survivorship bias: the dollar's reserve status is self-reinforcing, structurally irreplaceable, and threatened only at the margins by rivals who lack the deep capital markets, institutional credibility, and network effects that make dollar dominance self-perpetuating. The consensus does not deny that de-dollarisation sentiment is rising. It categorises that sentiment as aspirational rather than operational states that wish they could escape dollar dependency but cannot, because the exit costs exceed the entry costs of any available alternative.

"The consensus has confused the dollar's demonstrated resilience under prior stress with immunity to the qualitatively different stress it now faces. SWIFT weaponisation is not another stress event of the familiar kind. It is the moment the network effect became a coercion mechanism and that transformation changes every non-Western central bank's calculus about the cost of continued dependency."

The specific consensus claims that require stress-testing are the following. First: BRICS+ counter-architecture is technologically immature and years away from the scale needed to process meaningful trade volumes, making current de-dollarisation activity symbolic rather than structural. Second: China's yuan cannot replace the dollar as a reserve currency because the renminbi is not freely convertible and China's capital account controls prevent the open financial architecture that reserve status requires. Third: the 2022 Russian SWIFT exclusion demonstrated Western financial coercive capacity but did not produce a durable multilateral response countries adapted individually without creating systemic alternatives. Fourth: the Iran war's destruction of the primary sanctioned-supply network has weakened rather than strengthened the petroyuan architecture, setting de-dollarisation back by three to five years. Fifth: dollar share of global reserves, while declining from its 2001 peak of 73%, remains at approximately 58% comfortably dominant, with no credible successor in view.

Each of these claims contains a kernel of truth surrounded by reasoning that fails under the specific conditions of 2026. The cumulative sanctions events Russia in 2022, Iran in 2026, with Venezuela and North Korea as prior precedents have not merely inconvenienced target economies. They have delivered a universal lesson to every non-Western central bank simultaneously: dollar reserves held in Western custodian institutions can be frozen without notice, without judicial process, and without meaningful recourse. That lesson is being acted upon right now, in ways that move slowly enough to be systematically underestimated by analysts watching monthly reserve composition data and seeing only gradual change.

Where Analysis Fails

The Analytical Faultlines in the Consensus

The consensus view on dollar hegemony's durability contains six compounding analytical errors that collectively produce a picture significantly more optimistic than the structural evidence warrants.

Error One The Threshold Misread

SWIFT Weaponisation Changed the Incentive Structure Permanently Not Temporarily

Prior sanctions events Iran's partial SWIFT exclusion in 2012, North Korea's exclusion, Venezuela's financial restrictions were absorbed by target economies and produced limited systemic response because the affected states were geopolitically isolated, economically small, and widely perceived in the Global South as bearing some responsibility for their situation. Russia's 2022 SWIFT exclusion was categorically different in its systemic effects, not because Russia is larger, but because Russia is a G20 economy, a permanent UN Security Council member, and a state whose exclusion demonstrated that SWIFT can be weaponised against a major economy with significant global trade relationships. The lesson absorbed by every non-Western central bank was not "Russia is being punished for Ukraine." It was "any of us could be next, for any reason that Washington defines as sufficient." The 2026 Iran exclusion compounded this lesson with a second data point. Two SWIFT exclusions of major economies in four years transforms the risk model. It is no longer a theoretical concern for Beijing, New Delhi, Riyadh, and Brasília. It is a demonstrated operational reality with a repeating pattern.

Error Two The Alternative Threshold Fallacy

The BRICS Counter-Architecture Does Not Need to Replace SWIFT Only to Handle the Marginal Transaction

The standard Western dismissal of BRICS+ payment alternatives focuses on their inability to replace SWIFT at global scale: insufficient liquidity, limited currency convertibility, narrow bilateral coverage, technological immaturity. This framing sets the wrong threshold. A BRICS+ payment system does not need to process all global trade to structurally undermine dollar hegemony. It needs to handle a sufficient volume of the marginal transactions the ones that currently must pass through SWIFT because there is no alternative to create a credible parallel track. Price formation in commodity markets occurs at the margin. Reserve currency demand is determined at the margin. A system that can handle 15–20% of global trade outside SWIFT does not merely serve 15–20% of the market. It removes the coercive monopoly that makes SWIFT weaponisation effective. That removal changes every non-Western state's risk calculus about dollar dependency including states that never actually use the alternative system, simply because the alternative exists.

Error Three The Yuan Convertibility Non-Sequitur

China Does Not Need Full Convertibility to Achieve De-Dollarisation at Scale

The consensus argument that the yuan cannot become a meaningful reserve currency because China's capital account controls prevent free convertibility is technically accurate and strategically irrelevant to the specific de-dollarisation mechanism operating in 2026. The petroyuan architecture oil settled in yuan through bilateral commodity agreements does not require yuan convertibility. It requires yuan-denominated settlement accounts between counterparties who trust each other's bilateral relationship. Saudi Arabia selling oil to China in yuan and holding yuan balances in Chinese state bank accounts does not require opening China's capital account to global portfolio flows. It requires a bilateral settlement framework, a Chinese state bank guarantee, and a Saudi decision that yuan balances represent an acceptable store of accumulated export revenues. The question is not whether the yuan can replace the dollar as the world's freely convertible reserve currency. The question is whether yuan-denominated bilateral settlement can replace dollar-denominated settlement for a sufficient share of global commodity trade. These are fundamentally different thresholds, and the consensus is arguing against the wrong one.

Error Four The Frozen Reserves Lesson Is Irreversible

The $300 Billion Russian Reserve Freeze Cannot Be Undone and Every Central Bank Knows It

In March 2022, approximately $300 billion in Russian central bank foreign exchange reserves held in Western custodian institutions including the ECB, the Fed's New York branch, and the Bank of England were frozen. This was the largest sovereign asset confiscation in the post-WWII financial system's history. The legal basis remains contested under international law. The precedent it set was immediate and global: sovereign foreign exchange reserves denominated in dollars and held in Western custodian institutions are not, as the system had always assumed, legally protected assets of the sovereign depositor. They are political hostages available for seizure by the custodian states. Every non-Western central bank with significant dollar reserves including those of US allies has conducted the same risk assessment since March 2022. The behavioural response diversification into gold, yuan, and non-Western custodied assets is slow, non-linear, and systematically underreported in headline IMF reserve composition statistics. But it is operating and it is irreversible. No Western assurance can unlearn the lesson of March 2022.

Error Five The Petroyuan Setback Is Tactical, Not Strategic

The Iran War's Destruction of China's Sanctioned-Supply Network Accelerates, Not Delays, the Alternative Architecture

The consensus reads the Iran war's elimination of China's Iranian oil supply node a significant component of China's yuan-settled sanctioned-supply network as a strategic setback for petroyuan development that delays de-dollarisation by three to five years. This is a tactical reading applied to a strategic dynamic. China has lost the Iranian node. But the Iran war has simultaneously demonstrated to every Gulf oil producer that Chinese security guarantees cannot protect them from US military action, and that US-controlled financial infrastructure can be weaponised against their trading partners without warning. These two lessons operate in opposite directions: the first makes Gulf states less likely to rely on China for security, and the second makes them more motivated to build non-dollar settlement as insurance regardless of the security question. The Gulf states are diversifying their financial architecture not because they trust China but because they fear the United States. The Iran war has given them four additional reasons to fear US financial coercion and zero additional reasons to trust it.

Error Six The Gradual Decline Framing Obscures the Tipping Point Risk

Dollar Hegemony Does Not Erode Linearly It Has Non-Linear Failure Modes

The consensus tracks dollar reserve share as a slow, manageable decline from 73% in 2001 to 58% in 2026 a trend that, if extrapolated linearly, suggests decades before any crisis point is reached. This framing has two fatal flaws. First, the 58% figure is constructed using IMF COFER data that systematically undercounts non-reporting central banks primarily China, Gulf states, and BRICS+ members who do not fully disclose reserve composition. The true dollar share is likely 3–8 percentage points lower than reported. Second, and more importantly, dollar hegemony has non-linear failure dynamics. The network effect that sustains dollar dominance everyone uses dollars because everyone uses dollars is self-reinforcing on the way up and self-reinforcing on the way down. Once a critical mass of commodity trade migrates to non-dollar settlement, the incentive for remaining users to maintain dollar infrastructure declines rapidly. The tipping point is not visible in the gradual trend data that the consensus monitors. It becomes visible only after it has passed.

Suppressed Structural Drivers

The Forces Accumulating Below the Surface

Beyond the headline analytical errors, six structural dynamics are operating beneath the consensus narrative that will determine the dollar order's trajectory more decisively than any single policy announcement or bilateral agreement.

Suppressed Driver One

Central Bank Gold Accumulation as the Silent Reserve Diversification Signal

Global central bank gold purchases have run at record or near-record levels for four consecutive years: 2022, 2023, 2024, and 2025. The buying is concentrated in non-Western central banks China's PBoC, India's RBI, Turkey's TCMB, Poland's NBP, Singapore's MAS, and a cluster of Gulf central banks that have not publicly articulated their diversification rationale but whose actions speak precisely. Gold cannot be frozen. Gold has no custodian institution that can be pressured by Washington. Gold does not require convertibility or capital account openness. It is the reserve asset that every central bank can hold as insurance against the specific risk that March 2022 made undeniable. The gold accumulation trend is the most reliable leading indicator of the de-dollarisation psychology that precedes the operational infrastructure shift. It is systematically under-weighted in dollar hegemony analysis because gold holdings are reported quarterly with a lag, lack the narrative drama of a bilateral settlement announcement, and resist the linear modelling that consensus economists prefer.

Suppressed Driver Two

The mBridge Platform Operational Infrastructure Being Deliberately Underreported

Project mBridge a multi-CBDC (central bank digital currency) cross-border payment platform developed jointly by the BIS Innovation Hub, the Hong Kong Monetary Authority, the Bank of Thailand, the Digital Currency Institute of the People's Bank of China, and the Central Bank of the UAE achieved what the BIS called "minimum viable product" status in 2024. It has processed real-value cross-border transactions between participating central banks. Its significance is not yet in its volume which remains modest but in its architecture: it is a multilateral, central-bank-grade settlement infrastructure that operates entirely outside SWIFT. The BIS's own involvement provided Western analytical cover ("the BIS wouldn't build something threatening to dollar hegemony") that is analytically misleading. The BIS's mandate is financial stability, not dollar preservation. mBridge is real, operational, and being expanded. The question is not whether it works. It does. The question is the speed at which participating central banks are willing to route increasing volumes through it rather than SWIFT.

Suppressed Driver Three

The Sanctions Fatigue Lobby Inside Western Institutions

A growing constituency within Western financial institutions, multinational corporations, and European governments is actively arguing that the over-use of SWIFT weaponisation as a foreign policy tool is accelerating the de-dollarisation it is intended to prevent. This is not a fringe position. It is expressed in ECB working papers, in European Central Bank board member public statements, in multinational bank lobbying of the US Treasury, and in the public positions of European governments (most explicitly Germany, France, and the Netherlands) who have argued that secondary sanctions extraterritoriality is damaging European financial sovereignty. This internal Western dissent does not threaten dollar hegemony directly. But it constrains the United States' ability to extend SWIFT weaponisation further limiting future sanctioning to the most extreme cases precisely at the moment when the tool's effectiveness depends on its credible threatened use against a wider range of targets. The sanctions fatigue lobby is the inside-Western-institutions mirror image of the BRICS+ counter-architecture. Together they create a pincer movement on SWIFT's coercive monopoly.

Suppressed Driver Four

Trade Invoice Currency Shift The Least Visible and Most Structural Change

Global trade invoice currency data the currency in which exporters choose to denominate international trade contracts is the most structurally significant but least dramatically reported indicator of de-dollarisation. Dollar invoice share has historically run at approximately 50% of global trade, far exceeding the US share of global GDP (approximately 25%), reflecting the dollar's role as the primary vehicle currency for third-country trade (e.g., Brazilian soybeans exported to China, invoiced in dollars neither party uses domestically). A shift in invoice currency driven by bilateral commodity agreements denominated in yuan, ruble, rupee, or currency baskets is the operational mechanism through which de-dollarisation moves from central bank balance sheets to commercial trade flows. Data from China's cross-border trade statistics shows yuan-invoiced trade has increased from approximately 2% in 2015 to approximately 7–8% in 2025. This is slow. It is also directionally unambiguous, and the trend acceleration in 2022–2026 following each SWIFT weaponisation event is the clearest empirical signal of causal mechanism at work.

Suppressed Driver Five

The US Fiscal Deficit as the Dollar's Internal Structural Threat

Dollar hegemony's external threats BRICS+ counter-architecture, petroyuan, mBridge receive the majority of analytical attention. The internal structural threat receives less: the United States federal debt has crossed $35 trillion, with annual deficit running at approximately $1.8–2 trillion. Interest payments on the federal debt now exceed $1 trillion annually the largest single line item in the federal budget, exceeding defence spending. The dollar's reserve status is the mechanism that allows the United States to finance this deficit at interest rates below what any non-reserve-currency sovereign could sustain. If dollar reserve share declines materially reducing the captive demand for US Treasuries from foreign central banks the interest rate required to finance the deficit rises. Higher rates increase the deficit further. The feedback loop between declining reserve demand, rising yields, increasing deficits, and accelerating reserve diversification is the dollar's internal tipping-point mechanism, and it requires no external adversary to operate. It is already running at low speed. Any external shock a BRICS+ payment system achieving critical mass, a Gulf reserve reallocation can accelerate it to a speed the Federal Reserve cannot manage without inflationary monetisation.

Suppressed Driver Six

The Global South Legitimacy Shift Sanctions as Colonial Tool Narrative

In the Global South which comprises approximately 85% of the world's population and a growing share of global GDP Western financial sanctions are increasingly framed not as legitimate multilateral tools of international law but as instruments of colonial-era economic coercion deployed selectively against non-Western states. This framing was building since the Iraq sanctions of the 1990s. It was dramatically accelerated by the Russian sanctions of 2022 which countries representing 40% of global GDP refused to join and has been further solidified by the Iran war's post-IAEA-clearance strike sequence. The consequence is a legitimacy deficit that makes future SWIFT weaponisation politically more costly to execute, more diplomatically damaging with fence-sitter nations, and more motivating for alternative architecture investment. Dollar hegemony rests on consent as well as coercion. The consent is eroding faster than the coercive capacity. That asymmetry has a consequence that the consensus has not adequately modelled: the tool loses effectiveness before the infrastructure to replace it is fully built, creating a gap period in which neither the old order nor the new one is fully operative the most unpredictable and therefore most dangerous monetary transition environment.

The Defence Case

Why the Dollar Order Might Survive Intact

The structural case for dollar hegemony's durability is not merely self-serving Western ideology. It rests on genuine network effects, institutional depth, and competitor limitations that are not adequately represented in the most aggressive de-dollarisation narratives. A complete analysis requires honest accounting of the defence case.

Defence One The Depth Moat

US Capital Markets Are Incomparably Deeper Than Any Alternative

The US Treasury market is the world's largest and most liquid asset class at $26 trillion. The total US equity and bond market capitalisation exceeds $80 trillion. No alternative reserve currency system commands remotely comparable depth. China's bond market, at approximately $22 trillion, is theoretically large enough but its accessibility to foreign investors is constrained by capital controls, its liquidity in crisis conditions is untested, and its legal framework for foreign holder rights provides inadequate protection against political intervention. A global financial system that shifted reserve allocation to yuan-denominated Chinese government bonds would be accepting dramatically lower liquidity, higher political risk, and inferior legal protections relative to the status quo. Central banks understand this. It is a genuine constraint on de-dollarisation speed that the most aggressive forecasts underweight.

Defence Two The Dollar Debt Trap

$13 Trillion in Non-US Dollar-Denominated Debt Creates Inescapable Structural Demand

Approximately $13 trillion in bonds issued by non-US sovereigns, corporations, and financial institutions are denominated in dollars. These borrowers must service their debt in dollars regardless of their reserve preferences. This creates structural dollar demand that is entirely independent of reserve currency choice countries that would prefer to de-dollarise are forced to maintain dollar access and dollar liquidity to service existing obligations. The dollar debt trap is self-reinforcing: it creates dollar demand that validates the dollar's liquidity premium, which makes future dollar borrowing attractive, which deepens the trap. Escaping it requires either a coordinated global debt restructuring into non-dollar instruments which no institutional framework can deliver or a multi-decade deleveraging that compresses de-dollarisation timelines to a pace well below what the geopolitical narrative suggests.

Defence Three BRICS+ Fragmentation

China, India, Saudi Arabia, and Brazil Have Interests That Actively Conflict

The BRICS+ grouping is not a cohesive bloc with shared monetary interests. China wants yuan internationalisation and an architecture that routes commodity trade through Chinese institutions. India wants de-dollarisation that does not produce yuan dominance it prefers a multipolar currency system in which the rupee gains regional weight. Saudi Arabia wants dollar system insurance without abandoning the US security relationship. Brazil wants access to US capital markets and has a domestic dollar-denominated debt problem that makes de-dollarisation costly. Each of these national interests is partially compatible with the others and partially in conflict. The political coordination required to build a BRICS+ settlement unit that all four would actually use rather than each using their own bilateral alternatives is a genuinely difficult collective action problem that the geopolitical narrative consistently underestimates.

Defence Four The Legal Infrastructure

Dollar Contract Enforcement Has No Global Peer

International commercial contracts shipping, commodity trade, project finance, M&A default to New York law or English law, settled in dollars, with dispute resolution in New York or London courts. This is not mere convention. It reflects the genuine superiority of these legal systems in providing credible, predictable, enforceable contract protection for parties who do not share a domestic legal jurisdiction. Chinese courts cannot credibly enforce against US counterparties. The BRICS+ legal infrastructure for cross-border commercial dispute resolution does not exist at comparable depth. Any major shift away from dollar contract denominations requires a parallel shift in legal infrastructure that the counter-architecture has not addressed. This is a genuine, underappreciated constraint on the speed of de-dollarisation in commercial trade as opposed to central bank reserve allocation, where legal infrastructure matters less.

Defence Five US Adaptation Capacity

Washington Has Options It Has Not Yet Deployed

The dollar order has survived previous stress events through adaptation rather than mere inertia: the Plaza Accord managed yen appreciation, the 1998 crisis demonstrated Fed swap line capacity, the 2008 crisis produced dollar swap line expansion that reinforced dollar centrality in crisis conditions. Washington has not yet deployed its full adaptive toolkit in response to the de-dollarisation challenge. Options available but not yet used include: a formal multilateral agreement with Gulf states to continue dollar oil settlement in exchange for security commitments (a new Petrodollar Treaty); a coordinated G7 response to mBridge that creates a competing CBDC interoperability standard under Western institutional control; and a deliberate US current account adjustment that reduces the dollar's structural overvaluation and therefore the mercantile incentive for dollar reserve accumulation. None of these are certain. But the consensus that treats dollar hegemony as passive and reactive underestimates the system's capacity for deliberate defence.

Defence Six The Crisis Safe-Haven Paradox

Every Major Financial Crisis of the Last 30 Years Has Strengthened the Dollar

The 1997 Asian financial crisis, the 2008 global financial crisis, the 2020 COVID shock, and the 2022 Ukraine energy crisis all produced the same paradoxical result: capital fled to the dollar, strengthening it precisely during the period when its structural weaknesses were most visible. This is the safe-haven mechanism when global risk rises, the dollar's depth, liquidity, and institutional backing make it the default destination for capital that cannot afford to be wrong about where to shelter. De-dollarisation as a structural trend and dollar appreciation in a crisis are entirely compatible, even if they appear contradictory. The safe-haven mechanism provides the dollar system with a self-reinforcing shock absorber that the de-dollarisation narrative ignores. The next major global financial crisis whatever its source is as likely to produce a dollar strengthening episode as a de-dollarisation acceleration. The sequencing between these forces is one of the most important and least predictable variables in the entire analysis.

Tail Risks

Wild Cards What Could Shatter the Entire Analysis

The following events fall outside primary scenario modelling either genuinely low probability or systematically ignored despite visible evidence. Either category can prove decisive for the dollar order's trajectory.

⚠ Visible Risk Being Ignored

US Treasury Auction Failure Triggered by Coordinated Foreign Reduction

A US Treasury auction fails to reach its bid-to-cover ratio target not because of a sudden event but because the quiet diversification of foreign central bank holdings has removed a structurally significant share of captive bidders precisely as the US deficit requires record auction volumes. This is not a hypothetical adversarial attack. It is the arithmetic consequence of the trends already operating. A failed or weak auction at elevated yield triggers a market confidence event: yields spike further, dollar weakens, oil-denominated imports become more expensive, inflation rises, the Federal Reserve faces a policy paralysis between fighting inflation and supporting Treasury markets. The self-reinforcing loop activates. Markets would have seen the individual trend data points for years before the auction failure. They will still call it a Black Swan when it happens.

⚠ Visible Risk Being Ignored

Saudi Arabia Makes a Public Petroyuan Announcement

MBS holds a press conference announcing that Saudi Aramco will accept yuan settlement for Chinese oil purchases above a defined volume threshold a formal, public, announced policy rather than the quiet bilateral pilot currently operating. This single announcement would produce more immediate market impact than any infrastructure development: it signals the Gulf's largest oil exporter has judged the reputational cost of breaking with the dollar system to be lower than the coercion risk of maintaining it. The immediate consequence is a dollar sell-off and gold spike. The medium-term consequence is a queue of other Gulf states making similar announcements, since no GCC member wants to be the last one holding the dollar bag when the system reprices. The probability is low because MBS is cautious and the US leverage is real. It is not negligible because the structural motivation has never been higher.

⚠ Visible Risk Being Ignored

BRICS+ Member Sanctioned and Successfully Routes Around SWIFT

The United States sanctions a BRICS+ member state in a scenario where the political calculation leads Washington to apply maximum financial pressure and that state successfully continues commodity export operations by routing through mBridge, CIPS, and bilateral settlement frameworks within 60 days. The proof-of-concept event that demonstrates SWIFT exclusion is no longer functionally lethal triggers rapid institutional adoption by every non-Western central bank and trading institution that has been waiting to see the infrastructure tested under real conditions. The cascade adoption event compresses the de-dollarisation timeline from a decade to three to five years. The SWIFT tool's deterrence value collapses not because the US chose to abandon it but because its demonstrated ineffectiveness has removed its coercive credibility. This is the scenario the US Treasury's sanctions designers most fear and are least publicly prepared to address.

◈ Low Probability Catastrophic Impact

Dollar Confidence Crisis The 1971 Moment in Reverse

In August 1971, Nixon closed the gold window and ended the Bretton Woods system in a weekend announcement. The reverse event a dollar confidence crisis that forces the United States to accept a multilateral monetary framework it does not control would be the defining financial event of the 21st century. The mechanism is not a single dramatic decision but the accumulation of the trends described in this analysis reaching simultaneously: Treasury auction stress, Gulf reserve reallocation, SWIFT tool degradation, and a domestic US political crisis that prevents the fiscal adjustment that would stabilise the system. The Federal Reserve prints; inflation rises; foreign holders accelerate dollar exit; the loop closes. This is a 10–15 year horizon scenario with perhaps a 5–8% probability over that window. Its consequences dollar devaluation, forced multilateral reserve system negotiation, probable global recession would make the 2008 financial crisis appear moderate. It is being modelled by almost no institution outside the Bank for International Settlements, which does not publish its models.

◈ Low Probability Epoch-Altering

BRICS+ Commodity-Backed Settlement Unit Achieves G20 Adoption

A BRICS+ settlement unit backed by a basket of commodities (oil, gold, grain, rare earths) and operating through a multilateral clearing institution achieves sufficient legitimacy to be brought to the G20 agenda for discussion as a supplementary reserve asset. This is not the replacement of the dollar; it is the creation of a multilateral alternative analogous to the SDR but commodity-backed and institutionally controlled outside Western institutions. If discussed at G20 level, the political legitimisation process is underway regardless of its immediate adoption rate. The SDR took from 1969 to 1981 to become meaningfully used in international transactions. A commodity-backed BRICS+ unit would have stronger incentive alignment and a more urgent political motivation. The probability of G20 discussion within five years is approximately 10–15%. The probability of meaningful adoption within ten years, if the discussion occurs, is approximately 30–40%.

◈ Low Probability System-Altering

US Imposes Capital Controls in Response to Reserve Reallocation

Faced with accelerating foreign reserve diversification out of US Treasuries and a dollar that is weakening against a basket of emerging market currencies, a future US administration imposes capital controls limiting or taxing foreign sales of US Treasury securities, or imposing currency transaction taxes on dollar conversions as a defensive measure. This is precisely backwards from standard economic orthodoxy, which is why it is classified as low probability. But the political logic is available to a populist administration that frames capital controls as "stopping foreign governments from raiding our economy." Capital controls on dollar outflows would simultaneously halt the reserve diversification, accelerate the dollar's loss of reserve currency status, and trigger immediate retaliatory trade and financial measures from every affected country. It is the scenario that converts a manageable structural erosion into an acute systemic crisis through policy error historically the most common mechanism for financial system transitions.

⚠ Visible Risk Being Ignored

US Fiscal Crisis Forces Monetisation Before External De-Dollarisation Threshold

The external de-dollarisation narrative focuses on BRICS+ counter-architecture as the threat. The internal mechanism US fiscal trajectory forcing Federal Reserve monetisation at scale may reach its tipping point before external de-dollarisation achieves the critical mass the counter-architecture requires. A US political deadlock on fiscal adjustment (the combination of mandatory spending, tax cut extensions, and defence obligations that no bipartisan coalition can address), combined with an interest rate environment that raises debt service costs above $1.5 trillion annually, forces the Fed to monetise Treasury issuance at scale. The inflationary consequence erodes dollar purchasing power faster than the BRICS+ architecture develops. The dollar loses reserve status through internal debasement rather than external displacement. This is the scenario the consensus treats as too politically extreme to model. It is not. It is the straightforward arithmetic of current fiscal trajectories under plausible interest rate scenarios.

◈ Low Probability World-Altering

Bitcoin or Digital Asset Achieves Sovereign Reserve Status

A non-state digital asset most plausibly Bitcoin, given its fixed supply and censorship-resistant architecture achieves adoption as a significant component of sovereign foreign exchange reserves by a critical mass of non-Western central banks, providing a third alternative to both dollar reserves and yuan settlement. El Salvador's Bitcoin reserve adoption was symbolic. A Gulf sovereign wealth fund or BRICS+ central bank allocating 5% of reserves to Bitcoin as insurance against both dollar freezing and yuan convertibility risk would represent a qualitatively different event. The probability is low and the technical, custodial, and volatility obstacles are genuine. But the specific property that makes Bitcoin attractive to a sanctioned-economy central banker that it cannot be frozen by any custodian institution is precisely the property that the March 2022 precedent made most salient. The intersection of sovereign reserve management motivation and Bitcoin's specific censorship-resistance property is more consequential than most monetary analysts have modelled.

Scenario Modelling

Three Paths for the Dollar Order Through 2035

The following three scenarios represent the dominant probability mass for the dollar order's trajectory over the next decade. Each has defined preconditions observable against the watchpoints above, and each produces a qualitatively distinct global monetary environment.

Baseline Forecast · Assessed at 50%

Managed Erosion The Dollar Weakens but Does Not Break

Dollar global reserve share declines from approximately 58% to 48–50% by 2032, driven by a combination of central bank gold accumulation, petroyuan pilot expansion to 12–15% of Gulf-China bilateral trade, mBridge scaling to meaningful volume, and CIPS institutional expansion. SWIFT's coercive capacity degrades for any economy with BRICS+ relationships remaining effective against isolated states but losing its bite against connected economies. US Treasury yields rise structurally by 60–80 basis points above pre-de-dollarisation baseline as foreign captive demand declines, adding approximately $600–800 billion annually to US interest costs. The Federal Reserve manages the transition through a combination of QE restraint, bilateral swap line expansion with allied central banks, and domestic institutional demand cultivation.

The dollar does not collapse. It reprices downward in reserve share and upward in domestic interest cost, producing a sustained compression of US fiscal space that constrains foreign policy ambition over a decade. The global monetary system becomes genuinely multipolar dollar dominant but not hegemonic with the yuan, gold, and mBridge-facilitated bilateral settlement each handling significant shares of global trade. SWIFT weaponisation becomes reserved for the most extreme cases, limiting its deterrence value but preserving its technical availability. This scenario is uncomfortable for Washington but manageable. It does not produce a crisis. It produces a slow, grinding revision of US geopolitical capacity that compounds with every subsequent administration's fiscal choices.

50% · Uncomfortable but Manageable Transition
Contained Scenario · Assessed at 30%

Dollar Resilience The System Defends and Adapts

A combination of US policy adaptation, BRICS+ political fragmentation, and a major global financial crisis that triggers safe-haven dollar flows reverses the de-dollarisation momentum. The US executes a new Petrodollar Treaty with Gulf states, providing explicit security guarantees in exchange for continued dollar settlement commitments. mBridge expansion stalls as China and India fail to agree on governance and currency weight parameters. A global recession triggered by the Iran war's oil premium or by a Chinese real estate sector resolution drives emergency dollar demand that temporarily raises dollar reserve share above 60%. The Federal Reserve demonstrates dollar system resilience through coordinated swap line expansion with 30+ central banks, reinforcing dollar centrality in crisis management.

In this scenario, the SWIFT weaponisation episodes of 2022 and 2026 are retrospectively characterised as the high-water mark of dollar coercive overreach the moment that revealed the tool's limits and prompted corrective adaptation rather than continued escalation. US fiscal adjustment, facilitated by a bipartisan agreement that proves more politically achievable than the baseline scenario assumes, stabilises Treasury demand at current levels. Dollar reserve share stabilises at 55–58% through 2032. The counter-architecture exists but is marginalised a collection of bilateral alternatives used in edge cases rather than a systemic challenge. This requires three simultaneous favourable conditions: US policy competence, BRICS+ fragmentation, and a crisis that reinforces rather than challenges dollar safe-haven status.

30% · Requires Three Simultaneous Favourable Conditions
Accelerated Scenario · Assessed at 20%

Tipping Point The Transition Becomes Non-Linear

A combination of a Saudi public petroyuan announcement, a BRICS+ member successfully routing around SWIFT within 60 days of exclusion, and a US Treasury auction stress event occurring within an 18-month window triggers the non-linear failure dynamic that gradual trend analysis systematically misses. The proof-of-concept event SWIFT exclusion demonstrated as non-lethal triggers rapid institutional adoption of BRICS+ payment infrastructure. Dollar reserve share falls below 45% within three years. US Treasury yields rise above 6% without a corresponding economic justification, triggering the Fed's impossible choice between inflation and fiscal support. Gold crosses $5,000/oz. The global monetary system undergoes a disorderly transition toward multipolarity that no institutional framework has been designed to manage.

This scenario does not produce a single dramatic "dollar collapse" event. It produces a compressed, disorderly version of the managed erosion scenario the same destination reached in five years rather than fifteen, without the institutional adaptation period that makes managed transitions survivable. The consequences include: a significant dollar depreciation against commodity currencies; a US recession driven by the combination of higher import costs and higher debt service; a collapse of Western financial sanctions as a foreign policy tool; and a genuinely multipolar monetary system that reflects the distribution of global economic power rather than the post-WWII institutional settlement. This scenario's 20% probability is meaningfully higher than the approximately 2–3% that current market pricing of tail scenarios implies.

20% · Non-Linear Transition Significantly Underpriced
"The dollar's reserve status has survived every geopolitical challenge for eighty years, and the consensus is entirely correct that this track record deserves weight. But survivorship is not immunity. The specific stress now operating the demonstrated willingness to freeze sovereign reserves held in custodian institutions, applied twice in four years against major economies, watched by every non-Western central bank simultaneously is not a stress of the familiar kind. It is the moment the network effect became a coercion mechanism. And that transformation changes not merely the risk calculation of targeted states. It changes the risk calculation of every state that holds dollars in Western custody and understands that what has been done twice can be done again. The counter-architecture does not need to replace the dollar to end dollar hegemony. It needs only to make the dollar's coercive monopoly credibly contestable. That threshold, not the replacement threshold, is what SWIFT weaponisation has been steadily approaching. The question is not whether the marginal barrel moves. It has already started moving. The question is the speed of the cascade when it does."
